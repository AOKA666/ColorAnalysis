How the 12-season system works

Every palette balances three dimensions: temperature, value, and chroma. Temperature ranges from warm to cool, value from light to deep, and chroma from soft to bright.

The four classic seasons provide the broad direction. The twelve sub-seasons show which secondary quality matters most, such as Light Spring, Soft Autumn, Deep Winter, or Bright Winter.

02

The Spring and Summer families

Spring palettes are warm and fresh. Light Spring is airy and warm-neutral, True Spring is distinctly golden and clear, and Bright Spring combines warmth with high chroma and contrast.

Summer palettes are cool and blended. Light Summer is delicate and cool-neutral, True Summer is classically cool and soft, and Soft Summer moves toward neutral with smoke, taupe, and muted berry.

03

The Autumn and Winter families

Autumn palettes are warm and grounded. Soft Autumn is muted, True Autumn is richly warm, and Deep Autumn adds darkness and stronger contrast.

Winter palettes are cool and defined. Deep Winter emphasizes depth, True Winter emphasizes coolness, and Bright Winter combines cool-neutral color with clarity and high contrast.

04

How to use seasonal palettes well

Treat a palette as a relationship guide, not a box of mandatory colors. Start with neutrals, add three or four signature colors, and notice whether tonal or contrasting combinations repeat your natural coloring.

Printed or digital swatches vary by material, lighting, camera, and screen calibration. A professional draping session compares real fabrics under controlled light and can resolve close calls.

Frequently asked questions

Why are there different names for the same season?+

Color-analysis systems developed across different schools. Deep and Dark, or True and Cool, may overlap but are not always identical.

Are digital 12-season palettes accurate?+

They are useful for learning relationships, but screen color is not a substitute for physical drapes or product testing.

Can my season change?+

Natural coloring can shift with age and hair changes, but the underlying temperature and harmony usually remain relatively stable.

Color analysis is a styling tool, not a medical or identity assessment. Lighting, camera processing, display calibration, and personal preference all affect how color appears.
